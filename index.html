HISTORY OF THE BLACK COUNTRY CANALS

The canals are found in an area nicknamed the Black Country which is located around the Dudley Borough in the West Midlands, UK. The canal was opened in 1779, starting with the Stourbridge Canal. Eventually it connected through the Dudley Tunnel and through to the Birmingham Canal in 1792. It became the first canal that connected Birmingham to the Staffordshire and Worcestershire Canal (which also linked to the River Severn, River Trent, and River Mersey). The Dudley Canal was seen as part of a scheme to transport coal from coalfields near Dudley to Stourbridge. Limestone and ironstone were other potential cargos that became an effective industry otherwise known as the Industrial Revolution.

The canal systems of Britain acted as the highways we have today. The canals were used to transport goods, supplies and prisoners as an easier and accessable way around the country. The Midlands canal system which includes Birmingham and the Black Country was one of the busiest in Europe. Industries relied on the canal system to deliver these supplies and wares to sell at high prices. However once the introduction to trainlines and highways, the canal started to lose its grip on it transportation necessity.

The three main canal paths located within the Black Country are the Stourbridge Canal that begins in Stourbridge Town that branches to Fenns Pool on Pensnett Chase. Dudley No.1 path that makes its way through Dudley and Tipton (up to the famous Black Country Museum) and Dudley No.2 path that splits off from the prior path to make its way up to the Netherton Tunnel. All paths form at the Delph Locks, Located in Brierley Hill.
